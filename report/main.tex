\begin{abstract}
We study an iterative view expansion pipeline built around VGGT: starting from a few input images, we reconstruct a sparse scene, render a nearby unseen view, complete that view with an image generator, and rerun reconstruction with the newly synthesized image. The key comparison in this report is the generator itself, so we evaluate it primarily by \emph{faithfulness to the sparse target render}: on pixels already supported by geometry, does the generated image preserve the requested scene content or drift away from it? We compare a local FLUX model against Nano Banana 2 (\texttt{google/gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview}) on three scene-level examples (plant, Colosseum, Pyramid of Giza) and one object-only failure case (chair). On all three working scenes, Nano Banana shows substantially lower supported-pixel MAE than FLUX, which matches the qualitative impression that its generations are more faithful to the requested novel view. Loop-level exploration metrics such as orbit coverage can still favor FLUX, but those metrics conflate generation quality with planning aggressiveness and reconstruction stability. The core bottleneck remains the image generator: when the target sparse render is too incomplete, the generator drifts away from the scene even if the prompt is strong. We also document two practical findings that consistently helped: packaging reference views as a collage, and separating those views with white gutters so the model reads them as references rather than as a single composite scene.
\end{abstract}

\section{Method}
The pipeline starts from a small set of real views of the same scene. VGGT reconstructs a sparse point cloud and estimates cameras from those inputs \citep{wang2025vggt}. We then plan a new camera pose along the horizontal orbit that targets the largest currently unseen angular gap. From the sparse point cloud we render:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a \emph{target sparse render} at the desired novel pose, which is the direct geometric cue given to the image generator, and
  \item a \emph{source view render} at the nearest known pose, used as a fallback when the novel sparse projection is too empty to be informative.
\end{enumerate}

The generator receives the target sparse render (or the fallback source render when necessary) together with a single reference collage assembled from the available real images. We found this collage strategy more stable than passing many independent images through the generation interface. Adding explicit white gutters between the collage tiles reduced a recurring failure mode on landmarks, where the generator interpreted the reference sheet as a real panorama and duplicated the structure in the output.

The generated image is then appended to the image set and VGGT is rerun from images, not from accumulated point clouds. This distinction matters: each iteration rebuilds a fresh reconstruction from the current image set. We therefore report two different kinds of metrics:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{generator fidelity metric}: supported-pixel MAE between two images, computed only on pixels where the target render already has non-white geometric support,
  \item \textbf{loop diagnostics}: largest unseen gap and rotation error, which remain useful for understanding orbit growth and pose recovery but are not reliable standalone measures of generator quality.
\end{itemize}

The report intentionally focuses on 2D projections rather than 3D point-cloud screenshots. The actual decision loop uses renders and rerenders, and these views make the success or failure of each iteration much clearer than the raw point clouds, which were often visually noisy and misleading.

\section{Experimental Setup}
We run four-step iterative loops when possible. The main comparison uses three scene-level examples:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Plant}: small natural scene with foliage and moderate parallax,
  \item \textbf{Colosseum}: landmark scene with strong structure and wide-scale context,
  \item \textbf{Pyramid of Giza}: highly symmetric landmark, difficult because missing geometry can look like damage or internal voids.
\end{itemize}

We compare a local FLUX backbone \citep{blackforest2025flux2klein} against Nano Banana 2 served through OpenRouter \citep{openrouter2026geminiimagepreview}. The chair sequence is used separately as a limitation case rather than as a successful reconstruction benchmark because it lacks enough environmental context.

\section{Generator Comparison}

\subsection{Plant}
Figure~\ref{fig:plant-grid} shows the clearest qualitative improvement from changing the generator. Nano Banana produces more coherent plant structure, better color continuity, and fewer obviously broken leaves or pot fragments. In contrast, FLUX often keeps the scene roughly plausible but introduces more texture drift and structural wobble.

The fidelity metric supports that visual impression directly. On Plant, Nano Banana lowers supported-pixel MAE from 31.8 to 24.6. This means it preserves the pixels already specified by the sparse target render substantially better than FLUX. Plant is also the one scene where this generator improvement slightly helps the loop diagnostics as well.

\subsection{Colosseum}
Colosseum highlights a different trade-off. Earlier FLUX runs sometimes hallucinated multiple Colosseums when the reference collage was too tightly packed. The white-gutter collage improved this substantially, but FLUX still shows more leakage from the anchor sheet and more unstable large-scale geometry than Nano Banana. Nano Banana is better at preserving the idea of a single coherent landmark instead of inventing duplicated arena-scale structures.

The fidelity metric makes the generator advantage explicit even though coverage does not. Nano Banana lowers supported-pixel MAE from 30.1 to 19.4. So the model is much better at obeying the requested sparse view, even if the subsequent reconstruction still struggles to translate that into larger orbit expansion.

\subsection{Pyramid of Giza}
Pyramid is the hardest structured scene. The target sparse render frequently suggests a broken or hollow structure because the sparse points only cover part of the pyramid shell. We explicitly changed the prompt to name the \emph{Great Pyramid of Giza} and state that the visible gaps are due to missing points rather than a damaged monument. This improves the semantics of the generated image, especially for Nano Banana, which is less likely to produce a visibly collapsed pyramid.

Even with those prompt fixes and white-filled masking of unsupported areas, reconstruction remains poor. But again the generator fidelity metric favors Nano Banana clearly: supported-pixel MAE falls from 62.1 to 45.3. In other words, Nano Banana produces a more faithful pyramid image, yet that improvement is still not enough to overcome the underlying geometric ambiguity.

\subsection{Generator Fidelity Across Working Scenes}
Figure~\ref{fig:avg-metrics} now reports two related comparisons. The top row measures \emph{target to generated} fidelity: how much the generated image changes the pixels already specified by the sparse target render. The bottom row measures \emph{target to rerender} fidelity: after adding the generated image back into VGGT and rerendering the same pose, how much of that supported structure survives.

On the generator metric itself (top row), Nano Banana is better on every scene. The improvements are consistent rather than marginal:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Plant}: MAE drops from 31.8 to 24.6,
  \item \textbf{Colosseum}: MAE drops from 30.1 to 19.4,
  \item \textbf{Pyramid}: MAE drops from 62.1 to 45.3.
\end{itemize}

On average, Nano Banana reduces the target-to-generated supported-pixel error by about 28\%. This is the most defensible statement about generator quality in our setting, because it measures obedience to the geometric conditioning that the model was explicitly asked to preserve.

The rerender metric (bottom row) tells a slightly different but still favorable story for Nano Banana. After reconstruction, Nano Banana remains better on average, with MAE decreasing from 43.17 to 32.19. Plant is the only exception, where FLUX keeps a slightly better target-to-rerender match, but Nano Banana is clearly better on Colosseum and Pyramid.

By contrast, loop-level coverage metrics can still reward FLUX in some scenes because they are affected by step size, symmetry, and whether VGGT successfully localizes the new image afterward. Those diagnostics remain useful, but they should not be interpreted as proof that FLUX is the better image model.

\section{Limitations and Practical Findings}

\subsection{Object-Only Scenes Without Background}
The clearest limitation is the chair sequence in Figure~\ref{fig:chair-limit}. Both generators can synthesize plausible chair images from sparse conditioning, but the rerender after reconstruction remains poor. This is not mainly an image-generation problem. VGGT is designed for scenes, and the chair setup removes most of the surrounding background cues that stabilize camera estimation. Once that environmental support disappears, reconstruction collapses into white or unstable sparse points even when the generated chair itself looks reasonable.

This limitation is important for the report because it separates two notions of success:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{generator success}: the synthesized image looks like a chair,
  \item \textbf{reconstruction success}: that image helps VGGT recover consistent geometry and camera pose.
\end{itemize}
For the chair, the first can hold while the second fails.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{limitations_chair_generator_vs_reconstruction.png}
  \caption{Chair limitation case. For both generators, the target sparse render and final rerender remain poor even when the generated image itself is plausible. This supports the conclusion that background-poor single-object inputs are a poor match for a scene-centric reconstructor such as VGGT.}
  \label{fig:chair-limit}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Generator Is the Bottleneck}
Across all experiments, the dominant failure mode is not camera planning but weak generator anchoring. When the target sparse render contains enough structure, both generators can produce useful images. When it is too empty or too ambiguous, the generator invents content that is semantically plausible but geometrically unhelpful. This explains why the report emphasizes 2D target/generation/rerender triplets: they make it obvious when the generated image no longer matches the sparse geometry that asked for it.

There are also two practical bottlenecks in runtime and cost. VGGT is a real performance bottleneck because it must be rerun over an expanding image set at every iteration, so each loop repeatedly pays for camera estimation and sparse reconstruction. The main bottleneck, however, is still the GenAI model, because it dominates the final image fidelity. In practice, FLUX is operationally cheap: we can run it locally on a MacBook Pro M2 Max through MPS without per-request API charges. Nano Banana 2 is much more expensive. The three main Nano Banana report runs (Plant, Colosseum, Pyramid) cost about \$0.76 in total, and the broader OpenRouter activity snapshot for our experimentation showed about \$1.45 across 21 requests and roughly 61\,k tokens.

Three implementation tweaks were consistently useful:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{reference collage instead of many separate anchors}: easier to control and often more stable,
  \item \textbf{white gutters between collage tiles}: reduced duplicated structures by making the collage visually explicit,
  \item \textbf{strong scene naming in the prompt}: especially important for landmarks such as the Great Pyramid of Giza.
\end{enumerate}

These tweaks improve semantics, but they do not remove the core dependency on having a sufficiently informative sparse target render.

\section{Conclusion}
Replacing FLUX with Nano Banana makes the generator itself clearly better. Across Plant, Colosseum, and Pyramid, Nano Banana is superior on the most direct fidelity metric we kept in the report: it preserves the sparse target render more faithfully, with 28\% lower average target-to-generated MAE. Even after reconstruction, the target-to-rerender comparison still favors Nano Banana on average, with 25\% lower error. That result matches the qualitative impression throughout the report: Nano Banana drifts less, duplicates landmarks less often, and responds better to explicit scene prompts.

The main lesson is that the image generator, not VGGT alone, limits the pipeline. VGGT is a meaningful runtime bottleneck, but the dominant quality bottleneck is the GenAI model. Good-looking novel views are not enough; they must also remain geometrically anchored to sparse reprojection cues. The chair failure case makes the second key point explicit: without environmental context, a scene-centric reconstructor like VGGT is not a good match for isolated objects. From a practical perspective, FLUX remains attractive because it is cheap and local, whereas Nano Banana is better but materially more expensive.

Future work should therefore target stronger conditioning rather than stronger prompting alone: denser or more reliable sparse renders, generator interfaces that accept multiple structured references without ambiguity, and reconstruction models that are explicitly robust to object-only inputs.
